# Chapter 16: Breach Hymn

> **LUMEN THIEF - DEMOLITIONS TRAINING MANUAL (RESTRICTED):**
> *Subject: Controlled Destruction*
> *Instructor: Rho Sable*
> *Trainee: Avyanna Lagrange*
> *Summary: Making things fall is easy. Making them fall where you want is the job.*
> *Rule 1: Know what's on the other side of the wall.*
> *Rule 2: Precision is slower. Precision is the point.*
> *Rule 3: The shortcut always costs more than it saves.*
> *Addendum: If you're in a hurry, you're already losing.*

---

[The range is in the cargo hold's lower section-a reinforced bay that smells like ozone and old polymer and something sharp that catches in the back of Avyanna's throat. The walls are analog-hardened against corridor drift; someone has scratched "NO LATTICE INTERFERENCE" into the blast door frame.]

[Rho is already there when she arrives. He's setting up a simulation grid: holographic walls projected onto a metal frame, sensors embedded in the deck to track debris patterns.]

[He doesn't look up.]

Rho: You're early.

Avyanna: Elisira's lesson ran short.

Rho: Elisira's lessons never run short. She let you think it did.

[Avyanna blinks. He's right, probably. Everything Elisira does is deliberate.]

[Rho finishes calibrating the grid. The holographic walls flicker-gray and solid-looking, like real bulkheads. He steps back, wipes his hands on his thighs, and finally meets her eyes.]

Rho: Ready to learn how to break things?

Avyanna: I've broken things before.

Rho: Yeah. That's not the same.

---

[The range setup is simple: three simulated walls, each with different structural compositions displayed on a floating readout. Behind each wall, a cluster of red dots-simulated personnel. Hostiles. Civilians. Unknowns.]

[Rho hands her a pair of safety lenses. They're scratched and heavy.]

Rho: The goal isn't destruction. The goal is *access*. You want through a wall, not through whatever's behind it.

[Avyanna puts on the lenses. The world tints amber.]

Avyanna: What if what's behind it is hostile?

Rho: Then you figure that out *before* you blow the wall. Not after.

[He taps a control. The first simulated wall highlights-standard station composite, internal bracing visible in the overlay.]

Rho: First lesson: reading structure. Every wall tells you how it wants to fall.

---

[He walks her through the basics.]

[Stress points. Load-bearing vs cosmetic. The difference between "opens cleanly" and "collapses in a cascade that kills everyone in the next three rooms."]

[Avyanna listens. Her hands itch for something to hold-a wrench, a tool, anything that makes her feel less like a student and more like someone who belongs here.]

[Rho talks with his hands. Big gestures, precise despite their size. She notices the scars on his knuckles-different from Elisira's. These are burns, not cuts. Chemical or thermal, healed rough.]

Rho: See that brace? If you hit it wrong, the whole section torques. Clean breach becomes a hull compromise.

Avyanna: And if it's an emergency? If there's no time to read the structure?

[Rho's jaw tightens. Just for a second. The grin flickers-not armor snapping into place, but armor *slipping*. He catches it. Resets it. Too slow for casual.]

Rho: Then you're already in trouble. And trouble means people die.

---

[He sets up the first exercise: a simulated door, reinforced, with a lock mechanism and a timer.]

Rho: Standard Guild security door. You've got thirty seconds to get through it without triggering the alarm on the other side.

Avyanna: Thirty seconds isn't-

Rho: Yeah. It isn't. That's the point.

[He hands her a kit-small charges, placement tools, a detonator with a physical switch. No smart interface. Dumb reliability. A manifest tag on the case reads "ALLOCATION: TRAINING-VESPER APPROVED (GRUDGINGLY)."]

Rho: Go.

[Avyanna moves. Her hands are steadier than she expected. The mine taught her to work fast, to work small, to occupy as little space and attention as possible.]

[She finds the hinges. Places the first charge. Too high-she adjusts. The second charge goes on the lock mechanism. The timer ticks.]

[She triggers the detonator.]

[The door simulation fractures-but wrong. The debris pattern sprays backward, toward her position. Red warning lights flash across the safety lenses.]

Rho: Dead.

Avyanna: What?

Rho: You shaped the charge to push inward. But you didn't account for the lock's reinforcement. It redirected the blast.

[He taps the control. The simulation resets.]

Rho: Try again.

---

[She tries again.]

[And again.]

[The fourth attempt, the door opens cleanly. No debris spray. No alarm trigger. The red dots behind the door remain stationary-undisturbed.]

[Avyanna's hands are shaking slightly. Not fear. Adrenaline. The shard hums at the base of her skull-warm, attentive.]

Rho: Better.

Avyanna: It took four tries.

Rho: First time I did this drill, it took me eleven.

[She stares at him.]

Rho: [shrugging] What? I'm not naturally talented. I just practice until I stop being terrible.

[The honesty is disarming. Avyanna doesn't know what to do with it.]

---

[They move to the second exercise: a wall with civilians on the other side.]

[The simulation shows them as blue dots-clustered, unaware, positioned where a careless breach would kill them.]

Rho: Same wall. Different problem. How do you get through without casualties?

Avyanna: [studying the layout] Move them first?

Rho: Can't. They don't know you're coming. Moving them tips off the hostiles in the next section.

[Avyanna frowns. The geometry is wrong. Every angle she can see puts the breach zone too close to the civilians.]

Avyanna: Then you don't go through this wall.

[Rho's eyebrows lift. Interest, not approval-not yet.]

Rho: Go on.

Avyanna: You find a different entry point. One that doesn't put them at risk.

Rho: What if this is the only entry point?

Avyanna: Then you wait. Or you change the mission parameters. Or you-

[She stops. The heat at the base of her skull pulses-frustration, maybe. The shard doesn't like constraints it can't solve.]

Avyanna: I don't know.

Rho: That's the right answer.

[He taps the control. The simulation zooms out, showing the full layout-multiple entry vectors, timing windows, patrol patterns.]

Rho: There's always another way. The shortcut is the lie that says there isn't.

---

[They take a break. Rho produces a thermos from somewhere-coffee, or the ship's approximation of it. He pours two cups.]

[Avyanna takes hers. The warmth is grounding. The shard settles from frustrated to merely present.]

Rho: You're doing better than I expected.

Avyanna: [wary] Is that a compliment?

Rho: It's an observation. Compliments are Vesper's department.

[He drinks. The silence stretches. Comfortable, almost. The range hums with residual charge.]

Avyanna: Why do you do this?

Rho: Teach?

Avyanna: Demolitions. Breaking things. Making them fall.

[Rho's cup pauses halfway to his mouth. The grin doesn't fade, but something behind it shifts-like a door closing, then forcing itself open again.]

Rho: Because I'm good at it. And because someone has to be.

Avyanna: That's not an answer.

Rho: It's the one I've got.

---

[The next exercise is harder.]

[Rho sets up a scenario: a hull breach in progress, structural integrity failing, hostiles on one side and crew on the other. The timer starts at sixty seconds.]

Rho: You can seal the breach with a shaped charge-collapse the corridor, buy time for evacuation. But the collapse will kill the hostiles.

Avyanna: They're hostiles. They're trying to-

Rho: They're people. Maybe hired. Maybe coerced. Maybe desperate. Doesn't matter. They're still people.

[Avyanna's throat tightens.]

Avyanna: So what's the other option?

Rho: Slower. More precise. You shape the charge differently-collapse only the section you need, leave an exit route for them. Evacuation takes longer. More risk to the crew.

[He watches her. No guidance. No hints.]

Rho: Choose.

---

[Avyanna stares at the simulation. The timer ticks. Fifty-three seconds. Fifty-two.]

[The shard pulses-not heat, not cold. Something else. Pressure. Like it's waiting to see what she'll do.]

[She thinks of the mine. Of supervisors who made choices like this every day. "Acceptable losses." "Necessary sacrifices." Words that meant: *someone else pays so I don't have to.*]

(In the Kennel, I was the acceptable loss. I was the necessary sacrifice. I will not be the one who decides someone else is.)

Avyanna: The slower way.

Rho: Even with the risk?

Avyanna: Even with the risk.

[Rho nods. His face is unreadable.]

Rho: Do it.

---

[She fails the first attempt. The collapse is too wide-one of the blue dots winks out. Civilian casualty.]

[She fails the second attempt. The breach isn't sealed in time. The timer hits zero.]

[The third attempt, she gets it right. The hostiles escape through their exit route. The crew evacuates. The breach holds.]

[Avyanna's hands are shaking again. Not adrenaline this time. Something else.]

Rho: Slower is harder. Slower takes practice. But slower is what we do.

Avyanna: Why?

[The question comes out sharper than she intended. The shard's pressure hasn't faded-it's watching, listening, *waiting.*]

Avyanna: Why does it matter? If they're hostile-if they're trying to kill us-why does it matter if they have an exit?

---

[Rho is quiet for a long moment. The range hums. Somewhere in the ship, a ventilation system cycles.]

[When he speaks, the humor is gone. His voice is flat, careful, like he's reciting something he's said before-to himself, in the dark, when no one else was listening.]

Rho: Because shortcuts have a cost. And the cost is never the one you expect.

Avyanna: That's not-

Rho: I killed thirty-seven people with a shortcut.

[The words land like a physical weight. Avyanna's mouth closes.]

[Rho doesn't look at her. He's looking at the simulation grid-at the blue dots, the red dots, the debris patterns that mark where walls used to be.]

Rho: I was Compact military. Then rim. Then mercenary. Whatever paid, wherever the work was. I was good at it. Fast. Efficient.

[His hands curl around the thermos. The metal creaks.]

Rho: There was a situation. Andros Station. Under siege. Hostages in one section, hostiles in another. Command said we had a window-forty seconds to breach, extract, and evac before the station's core went critical.

Avyanna: [quiet] What happened?

Rho: I took the shortcut. Shaped charge through the main corridor. Fast. Clean. The hostiles went down. The window held.

[A beat.]

Rho: Except the corridor connected to a secondary habitation module. Civilians. Families. The shockwave killed them before they knew we were there.

[Avyanna's chest tightens. The shard goes cold-not pain, but something like recognition. Like it knows this shape of grief.]

Rho: Thirty-seven people. Twelve of them were children.

---

[The silence stretches. The range's ambient hum fills the space between them.]

[Avyanna doesn't know what to say. In the mine, people died all the time. You learned not to look. Not to count. Not to carry the weight of faces you couldn't save.]

[But Rho carries them. She can see it now-in the set of his shoulders, the tightness around his eyes, the way his jokes deflect before anyone can ask.]

Avyanna: The crew knows?

Rho: Elia knows. Elisira figured it out. Vesper has access to records she shouldn't. Jalen... Jalen lost people too. Different way. Same weight.

Avyanna: And they still-

Rho: They still trust me.

[He says it like he doesn't understand it. Like every day he wakes up expecting them to finally realize what he is.]

Rho: Elia gave me a rule. *Precision is the only absolution.* She didn't forgive me. She just... kept trusting me anyway. Made me earn it.

[He looks at Avyanna. Really looks-not the casual assessment of a teacher, but something rawer. Someone deciding how much of themselves to show.]

Rho: That's why we choose the slower way. That's why shortcuts cost more than they save. Because the people behind those walls-the blue dots, the red dots, whoever they are-they're not numbers. They're faces you'll carry forever.

---

[Avyanna's throat is tight. The heat at the base of her skull has shifted-not cold anymore, but not warm either. Present. Witnessing.]

Avyanna: I'm sorry.

Rho: [shaking his head] Don't be. Just... remember.

Avyanna: Remember what?

Rho: That the shortcut always lies. It says it's faster. It says it's cleaner. It says the cost is acceptable.

[He sets down the thermos. His hands are steady again-the mask reassembling, the armor clicking back into place.]

Rho: The cost is never acceptable. It's just the one you haven't paid yet.

---

[They run through two more exercises. Avyanna's focus is sharper now-not just technical, but moral. She reads the simulations differently. The blue dots aren't abstractions anymore. They're faces she hasn't met yet.]

[Rho's teaching shifts too. Less joking. More direct. He points out her mistakes without softening them, but he also names what she's doing right.]

Rho: Good. You're thinking about the exit before you think about the entry.

Avyanna: You said there's always another way.

Rho: There is. The trick is being patient enough to find it.

---

[At the end of the session, Rho packs up the simulation grid. The cargo hold returns to its default state-crates, cargo nets, the quiet hum of environmental systems.]

[Avyanna helps without being asked. The work is familiar-lifting, stacking, fitting pieces into spaces where they belong. The mine taught her this much.]

Rho: Same time tomorrow?

Avyanna: If you want.

Rho: I want.

[He pauses, one hand on a crate.]

Rho: Avyanna.

[She looks up.]

Rho: You asked why I do this. Breaking things. Making them fall.

[His voice is lighter now-the armor back, the jokes hovering at the edge. But underneath, something true.]

Rho: I do it because I already know what happens when you do it wrong. And I'd rather teach someone to do it right than watch them learn the way I did.

---

[Later, in the corridor outside the cargo hold, Avyanna pauses.]

[The shard hums-low, steady. Not pain. Not pressure. Just... presence. Acknowledgment.]

(He carries thirty-seven faces.)

[The thought settles into her like a new weight. Not crushing. Just there.]

(I carry the Kennel. He carries the corridor. Jalen carries Kettering's Rest. Everyone on this ship carries something.)

[She thinks of Elia's rule: *Precision is the only absolution.*]

[It doesn't sound like forgiveness. It sounds like a job. Something you do every day, whether you feel like it or not. A discipline instead of a pardon.]

(Maybe that's what floors are. Not absolution. Just... the work that makes you worth trusting.)

[The heat along her spine spreads-warmer now. Agreement, maybe. Or just the shard learning alongside her.]

[Avyanna walks back toward her bunk. The ship hums. Somewhere ahead, someone is arguing about the coffee machine again.]

[She almost smiles.]

## Coda - Rho's Ledger

[0300. The cargo hold is empty. The simulation grid is packed away. The deck plates are cold under Rho's boots.]

[He sits on a crate in the dark, datapad in hand. The screen's glow is the only light-blue and clinical, washing out the color in his face.]

[The file is encrypted. Three layers. Cinnamon could break it-she's broken harder-but she won't. Trust boundary. Some doors stay closed because someone asks, not because they're locked.]

**LEDGER - PERSONAL - RHO SABLE**

[The entries are names.]

[Thirty-seven of them at the top. Andros Station. The corridor. The shortcut that wasn't.]

[Below that: others. People who died because he was fast instead of careful. People who died because he followed orders. People who died because he was young and stupid and believed that "acceptable losses" was a real thing.]

[He doesn't read the names. He knows them. They're carved into the back of his skull, behind his eyes, in the place where dreams should be.]

[Tonight, he adds a new entry. Not a name. A number.]

**Days since last shortcut: 847**

[He stares at it. The number feels fragile. Tomorrow it will be 848. Then 849. Then a thousand, maybe. Or zero, if he screws up.]

[The jokes are armor. Everyone knows that. Rho knows they know. It doesn't matter. The armor works because it gives people something to react to-something easier than the truth.]

[The truth is this: the ledger is discipline. The count is work. Every day he doesn't take the shortcut is a floor under someone else's feet.]

*Precision is the only absolution.*

[He'd laughed when she said it. A short, bitter sound. "I'm not looking for absolution," he'd told her.]

[And Elia-small and calm and carrying her own weight of faces-had just looked at him.]

*"Then what are you looking for?"*

[He still doesn't have an answer. But he has the ledger. He has the count. He has tomorrow, and the day after, and every day after that where he doesn't take the shortcut.]

[That's not absolution. That's not forgiveness. That's just... staying alive long enough to do the work.]

---

[Rho closes the datapad. The cargo hold goes dark.]

[Somewhere in the ship, someone laughs. Jalen, probably-or Nyx, if they're feeling bold. The sound is muffled, distant, real.]

[He sits with it for a moment. The laughter. The hum of the ship. The weight of the faces he carries.]

[Then he stands. Rolls his shoulders. Lets the grin slide back into place like a familiar mask.]

[Tomorrow, he teaches Avyanna how to make a wall fall without hurting anyone behind it. Tomorrow, the count goes to 848.]

[Tonight, he adds the new number to the ledger and doesn't sleep.]

---

[The next morning, Avyanna finds Rho in the galley with Cinnamon's interface open on a datapad.]

[Numbers scroll across the screen-percentages, curves, compound functions that make her head hurt.]

Avyanna: [cautious] What's that.

Rho: [not looking up] Math.

Avyanna: I know that. What kind.

Rho: The kind that keeps people enslaved.

[He taps the screen. A debt contract fills the display-dense legalese wrapped around a single interest rate.]

Rho: You know what this number means?

Avyanna: [reading] Twenty-two percent annual interest.

Rho: No.

[He looks at her. The mask-grin is gone.]

Rho: It means they never intended you to pay it off.

[Avyanna's stomach drops.]

<CINNAMON.EXE// DEMONSTRATION AVAILABLE: compound interest visualization | RECOMMEND: show her the curve>

[The screen shifts. A graph appears-a line that starts flat, then curves upward like a scream.]

Rho: This is what twenty-two percent does over time. Every year, you owe more. Every payment you make barely touches the principal.

Avyanna: But if I just work harder-

Rho: No.

[He says it flat. Final.]

Rho: You can't outwork compound interest. That's the point. They built the math to be unbeatable.

<CINNAMON.EXE// CALCULATION: at 22% annual interest, original debt of 50k credits | TIME TO DOUBLE: 3.5 years | TIME TO QUADRUPLE: 7 years | ASSUMING MINIMUM PAYMENTS: never resolves>

[Avyanna reads the numbers. Her hands go cold.]

Avyanna: [quiet] I would have died there.

Rho: Yes.

Rho: Or your kids would've. Or their kids. That's the design.

[He swipes to a different screen-a cleaner contract, different numbers.]

Rho: This is what non-predatory lending looks like. Five percent. Fixed term. No compound interest on missed payments.

Rho: The math doesn't trap you. It just... exists.

Avyanna: The mine didn't teach us about interest rates.

Rho: Of course they didn't. If you understood the math, you'd understand you were being robbed.

<CINNAMON.EXE// PHILOSOPHY: innumeracy is a control tool | OBSERVATION: people who can't calculate percentages can't argue with debt claims | STRATEGY: teach the math, break the control>

[Avyanna stares at the graphs.]

Avyanna: So if someone won't show me the math...

Rho: They're lying.

Rho: Or they're too embarrassed to admit they don't understand it either.

[He pulls up another screen-shaped charge calculations, blast radius formulas, safety margins.]

Rho: This is how I plan breaches. Math. Precision. I can't afford to guess.

Rho: If I get the angle wrong by five degrees, the blast goes through the wall and kills someone behind it.

Rho: If I get the compound wrong by ten percent, the charge doesn't breach at all and we're trapped.

[He looks at her.]

Rho: Numbers don't lie. They don't care about your story. They just... are.

Rho: That's why they're powerful. And that's why people who want to control you make sure you never learn them.

Avyanna: [slow, tasting it] So learning math is...

Rho: Leverage.

<CINNAMON.EXE// LESSON PLAN AVAILABLE: basic percentages, compound interest, probability, statistical literacy | ESTIMATED TIME: 6 hours distributed | OUTCOME: quantitative ammunition against exploitation>

Avyanna: Cinnamon wants to teach me.

Rho: Good. Let her.

Rho: Percentages first. Then compound interest. Then probability-so you know when someone's selling you a bad bet.

Avyanna: And then I can...

Rho: Argue. Prove. Calculate your own risk instead of trusting theirs.

[He closes the datapad.]

Rho: They kept you innumerate for a reason. We're fixing that.

[Avyanna feels something shift. Like a lock she didn't know existed just... opened.]

Avyanna: [quiet] Thank you.

Rho: [grin sliding back] Don't thank me yet. Cinnamon's a brutal teacher. She'll make you show your work.

<CINNAMON.EXE// AFFIRMATIVE: precision is required | NO SHORTCUTS | UNDERSTANDING > MEMORIZATION>

[Avyanna almost laughs. Almost.]

Avyanna: I think I can handle that.

Rho: Good.

[He stands, stretches, lets the dad-joke energy return like a comfortable coat.]

Rho: Now go eat breakfast. Can't learn math on an empty stomach. That's just bad engineering.

[Avyanna watches him go. The datapad stays on the table, numbers still scrolling.]

[She picks it up. Reads the compound interest curve again.]

[This time, she understands what she's looking at.]

[This time, the numbers are hers.]

*End Chapter 16*
