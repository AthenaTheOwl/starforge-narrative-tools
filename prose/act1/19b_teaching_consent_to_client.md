# Chapter 19b: Teaching Consent to Client

> **[SCENE - OCCURS POST-CH19, AFTER CONTRACT COMPLETION]**
> *Location: Station meeting room*
> *Present: Avyanna (observing), Vesper, problematic client*
> *Purpose: Witnessing boundary-setting with difficult clients, learning to enforce scope*

---

[The client is unhappy. That much is obvious from their body language-arms crossed, jaw tight, the particular tension of someone who's used to getting what they want and isn't getting it now.]

[They sit across from Vesper in the same meeting room where Avyanna watched the last contract negotiation. This is the follow-up. The delivery. The moment when evidence changes hands and money changes accounts.]

[Except the client wants more than what they contracted for.]

[Avyanna sits beside Vesper, one chair back. Witness. Observer. Learning what happens when boundaries get tested after the work is done.]

---

Client: [tense] You retrieved the financial records. Good. But I need more.

Vesper: [neutral] More was not in the contract.

Client: [leaning forward] While you were in their system, you must have seen other files. Personnel records. Communications. Internal memos. I need those too.

Vesper: [flat] No.

Client: [frustrated] I'm willing to pay-

Vesper: [interrupting] I don't care what you're willing to pay. We had a contract. The contract specified financial records only. We delivered financial records only. That's the transaction.

Client: [pushing] You were in their system. You had access. It would have taken five minutes to copy additional files-

Vesper: [cold] Five minutes that were outside contract scope. Five minutes that would have constituted theft rather than evidence retrieval. Five minutes that would have compromised our legal standing and our Guild reputation.

---

[The client's expression hardens.]

Client: [calculated] I could report that you refused to fulfill the full scope of service-

Vesper: [pulling out her datapad] Please do. Here's the contract. Guild-certified. Every term we discussed, documented in plain language. Nowhere does it say we retrieve anything beyond specified financial records.

[She projects the contract holographically. Lets the client read it.]

Vesper: [precise] You're attempting to retroactively expand scope. That's not how contracts work. You got exactly what you paid for. Nothing less. Nothing more.

Client: [shifting tactics] What if I offered a new contract? Right now. To retrieve those additional files.

Vesper: [meeting their eyes] I'd decline.

---

Client: [surprised] You'd decline paid work?

Vesper: [firm] I'd decline work from a client who tries to coerce scope expansion after delivery. That behavior is a red flag. It suggests you'll do it again. And again. Until we compromise our ethics to satisfy your demands.

[She stands.]

Vesper: [to the client] Here's what happens now. You accept the evidence we delivered per contract. You pay the agreed amount. We part ways. Or you refuse, and I file a dispute with the Guild, which will side with us because we have documentation proving we fulfilled every contracted term.

Vesper: [leaning forward slightly] Your choice. But choose quickly. I have other clients who respect boundaries.

---

[The client glares. Avyanna watches, tense. In the mine, this is where violence would happen. Where authority would assert itself through force. Where the person with less power would lose.]

[But this isn't the mine. This is Guild space. And Vesper has contracts. Documentation. Receipts.]

[The client pulls out a credit chit. Transfers the agreed payment. The transaction completes with a soft chime.]

Client: [standing, bitter] I'll remember this.

Vesper: [unbothered] Good. Then you won't hire us again. We prefer clients who understand consent.

[The client leaves. The door hisses shut behind them.]

---

[Vesper sits back down. Lets out a slow breath.]

Vesper: [to Avyanna] What did you observe?

Avyanna: [carefully] They tried to change the contract after you'd already done the work.

Vesper: [nodding] What else?

Avyanna: [thinking] They tried multiple tactics. Direct request. Guilt. Threat. Bribery. When one didn't work, they tried another.

Vesper: [approving] Yes. That's manipulation. People who don't respect boundaries will test every angle until they find one that works or until you shut them down completely.

---

[She closes the contract file.]

Vesper: [measured] That client will probably complain about us. They'll say we were inflexible. Uncooperative. That we refused reasonable requests.

Vesper: [meeting Avyanna's eyes] They'll be right about one thing. We were inflexible. Because flexibility about scope is how you end up doing illegal work, compromising your ethics, and destroying your reputation one "small exception" at a time.

Avyanna: [quiet] You said no. Multiple times. They kept pushing.

Vesper: [firm] And I kept saying no. Because no is a complete sentence. It doesn't need justification. It doesn't need softening. It just needs to be clear and consistent.

---

[Vesper stands, gathers her things.]

Vesper: [to Avyanna] You're going to encounter clients like this. People who think they can pressure you into compromising. People who think your boundaries are negotiable if they pay enough or threaten enough or guilt enough.

Vesper: [meeting her eyes] Here's what you do. You hold your boundary. You reference the contract. You document everything. And if they continue pushing, you walk away.

Avyanna: [uncertain] What if I need the money?

Vesper: [flat] You'll always need money. But if you compromise your boundaries for money once, you teach people that your boundaries are for sale. And then every future client will try to buy them.

---

[They leave the meeting room. Walk through the station corridor. Around them: normal station life. People who don't know that a contract just held against pressure. That boundaries just won without violence.]

Avyanna: [after a moment] In the mine, we didn't have contracts.

Vesper: [nodding] I know.

Avyanna: [continuing] They'd tell us to do something. We'd do it. Then they'd add more tasks. More hours. More danger. And if we said no, we got punished.

Vesper: [stopping, turning to face her] That's why contracts matter. That's why documentation matters. That's why saying no matters.

[She taps her datapad.]

Vesper: [precise] The mine operated on power imbalance and information asymmetry. You had no documentation. No proof of what was agreed. No recourse when they changed terms.

Vesper: [meeting Avyanna's eyes] Here, we document everything. Every term. Every boundary. Every transaction. So when someone tries to change the agreement, we have proof of what was actually agreed to.

Vesper: That's not just good business. That's survival.

---

[They return to the ship. Vesper files the contract completion report. Avyanna sits in the common room, processing.]

[Vesper said no. The client pushed. Vesper held the boundary. The client paid anyway because the contract was clear. No violence. No coercion. Just documentation and refusal.]

(This is what power looks like when you have receipts. When you have records. When you have proof that you did exactly what you agreed to do and nothing more.)

[Rho enters, sees her thinking.]

Rho: [sitting] Vesper told me about the client. The one who wanted scope expansion.

Avyanna: [nodding] She said no. Multiple times.

Rho: [grinning] Yeah. Vesper's a master of the strategic "fuck no." It's beautiful to watch.

---

Avyanna: [careful] What if the client had been dangerous? What if saying no meant... violence?

Rho: [sobering] Then we wouldn't have taken the contract in the first place. We vet clients. We assess risk. We don't put ourselves in situations where saying no gets us killed.

Rho: [leaning forward] But here's the thing. Most people don't escalate to violence when you say no. They escalate to manipulation. Guilt. Pressure. Threats that are mostly bluster.

Rho: [meeting her eyes] And if you hold your boundary through all of that? They usually back down. Because people who rely on manipulation aren't usually willing to risk actual consequences. They want easy compliance. Not a fight.

---

[Avyanna processes that. Most people back down when boundaries hold.]

Avyanna: [quiet] The mine taught me that saying no was dangerous.

Rho: [nodding] Because in the mine, it was. The mine had all the power. You had none. Saying no meant punishment because they could enforce that punishment without consequence.

Rho: [gesturing around the ship] Here, power is more balanced. Vesper has contracts. Documentation. Guild backing. She can say no because she has structural support for her boundaries. The client can't punish her without facing Guild sanctions.

Rho: [to Avyanna] You're learning that you can have that too. Structural support. Documentation. People who'll back your boundaries. It's not just you versus the world anymore.

---

[Avyanna sits with that. Structural support for boundaries. Not just personal strength, but systems that protect refusal.]

[She thinks about the contract. About Vesper saying no. About the client leaving angry but paying anyway. About documentation as power.]

(This is what the mine didn't want us to know. That boundaries are enforceable when you have proof. When you have support. When you have systems that back your right to refuse.)

[The shard at the base of her skull is warm.]

(A warm pulse at the base of her skull: no is complete; no is protected.)

[She stands. Tomorrow, there will be another contract. Another client. Another opportunity to watch boundaries hold or bend. Another lesson in how consent operates in commercial contexts.]

[And slowly, incrementally, she's learning that saying no doesn't have to end in violence. Sometimes it just ends in contracts fulfilled and payments transferred and clients walking away disappointed but compliant.]

[Sometimes no is enough.]
